Injuries & Recovery

Interactive Anatomical Reference
Muscle Soreness (DOMS)
Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness is normal 24-72 hours after intense exercise. Rest, hydration, light stretching, foam rolling, and massage help. Active recovery like walking accelerates healing. Usually resolves in 2–4 days.
Muscle Cramps
Stay hydrated and maintain electrolyte balance (sodium, potassium, magnesium). Stretch affected muscle gently and hold for 30 seconds. Apply heat or ice. Reduce workout intensity temporarily. Ensure proper warm-up before exercise.
Stiff Joints
Warm up properly before exercising with dynamic stretches. Use static stretching post-workout. Stay active with light movement throughout the day. Consider foam rolling and mobility work. Heat therapy can help loosen tight joints.
Minor Bruises
Apply ice for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours for first 48 hours. Elevate the area if possible. Use compression wrap. Avoid massaging the bruise initially. Usually heals in 1-2 weeks. If pain worsens or bruise spreads significantly, consult doctor.
Blisters
Do not pop unless painful or interfering with activity. Clean with antiseptic if it pops naturally. Apply antibiotic ointment and bandage. Prevent with proper footwear, moisture-wicking socks, and addressing hot spots early. Heals in 3-7 days.
Shin Splints
Rest from impact activities for 2-3 weeks. Ice shins for 15-20 minutes after activity. Strengthen calf and shin muscles with toe raises and alphabet exercises. Ensure proper running shoes with arch support. Gradually increase training intensity. Stretch calves regularly.
Tendonitis (General)
Rest the affected area for 2-4 weeks, apply ice for 15-20 minutes 3-4 times daily. Use anti-inflammatory medication as directed. Gradually return to exercise with reduced intensity. Physical therapy with eccentric exercises highly beneficial. Avoid complete immobilization. May take 6-12 weeks for full recovery.
Ankle Sprain (Mild-Moderate)
RICE protocol: Rest, Ice (20 min every 2-3 hours), Compression wrap, Elevate above heart. Avoid weight-bearing for 48-72 hours. Use ankle brace when returning to activity. Perform balance exercises on wobble board. Strengthen with resistance band exercises. Most heal in 2-6 weeks.
Plantar Fasciitis
Stretch plantar fascia and Achilles tendon multiple times daily. Roll foot over frozen water bottle. Wear supportive shoes with arch support. Use night splints. Reduce impact activities temporarily. Strengthen foot muscles with towel scrunches. May require 6-18 months for full recovery. Consider orthotic inserts.
IT Band Syndrome
Reduce running volume immediately. Ice outer knee for 15-20 minutes post-activity. Foam roll IT band and hip muscles daily. Strengthen hip abductors and glutes with clamshells and side leg raises. Stretch hip flexors. Gradually return to running with shorter distances. May take 4-8 weeks to resolve.
Rotator Cuff Strain
Rest shoulder and avoid overhead activities for 2-4 weeks. Ice for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours initially. Perform gentle pendulum exercises after 48 hours. Progress to resistance band exercises focusing on external rotation. Strengthen scapular stabilizers. Physical therapy recommended. Full recovery may take 6-12 weeks.
Hamstring Strain
RICE protocol immediately. Avoid stretching aggressively initially. Begin gentle stretching after 48-72 hours. Strengthen with Nordic hamstring curls once pain subsides. Gradually increase running intensity. Ensure proper warm-up. Grade 1 strains heal in 2-3 weeks, Grade 2 in 4-8 weeks. Rushing recovery risks re-injury.
ACL / MCL Tear
SEEK IMMEDIATE MEDICAL ATTENTION. This requires professional diagnosis via MRI and potentially surgical reconstruction. Do not attempt to exercise or bear weight. Follow RICE protocol until medical evaluation. Rehabilitation takes 6-12 months post-surgery. Follow doctor's rehabilitation plan strictly. High risk of re-injury without proper recovery.
Severe Ligament Sprain (Grade 3)
URGENT: Get immediate medical evaluation. Complete ligament tear may require surgical repair. Do not bear weight on affected limb. Use crutches and immobilization as directed. Follow RICE protocol. Consult orthopedic specialist within 24-48 hours. Recovery may take 3-6 months with surgery, longer without. Physical therapy essential.
Stress Fracture
SEEK MEDICAL EVALUATION IMMEDIATELY. X-ray or MRI needed for diagnosis. Complete rest from impact activities for 6-8 weeks minimum. May require walking boot or crutches. Non-weight bearing initially. Calcium and vitamin D supplementation. Address underlying causes (overtraining, nutrition, biomechanics). Gradual return to activity only after medical clearance.
Acute Nerve Injury
SEEK IMMEDIATE MEDICAL CARE. Do not apply pressure to affected area. Immobilize if possible. Go to emergency room for full neurological evaluation. May require imaging (MRI, nerve conduction studies). Treatment depends on severity and location. Some nerve injuries require surgical intervention. Follow-up with neurologist essential.
Herniated Disc
URGENT MEDICAL ATTENTION. Especially if experiencing numbness, tingling, or weakness in legs. MRI required for diagnosis. Conservative treatment: rest, physical therapy, pain management. May require epidural steroid injections. Surgery needed if severe symptoms or neurological deficits. Avoid heavy lifting and twisting. Recovery varies: 6 weeks to 6 months.
Complete Muscle Tear
IMMEDIATE MEDICAL EVALUATION REQUIRED. Often requires surgical repair depending on muscle and tear severity. Immobilize affected area. Apply ice. Elevate if possible. Do not attempt to use muscle. MRI and ultrasound needed for diagnosis. Post-surgery rehabilitation takes 3-6 months. Risk of permanent weakness if not treated properly.
Severe Concussion
EMERGENCY ROOM IMMEDIATELY if loss of consciousness, severe headache, vomiting, confusion, or worsening symptoms. Complete physical and cognitive rest required. No screens, reading, or stimulation initially. Gradual return-to-play protocol under medical supervision. May take weeks to months. Second impact syndrome can be fatal - do not return to sports without medical clearance.
⚠️
Emergency Protocol
If experiencing severe pain, loss of consciousness, difficulty breathing, chest pain, suspected fracture, severe bleeding, or any life-threatening symptoms — seek immediate medical attention. Do not delay.
Call 102 (Ambulance) Call 108 (Emergency)
